Understanding Absolute Infinity - Part 2

  • Understanding Absolute Infinity - Part 2: This section presents part two of Leo Gura's lecture on understanding absolute infinity. The focus is on the historical connections and discoveries associated with absolute infinity, particularly the work of Georg Cantor. Considered the father of set theory, Cantor was interested in infinity, exploring and comparing the sizes of different infinities. He discovered and proved that there are different sizes of infinity; for example, there are the same number of even numbers as whole natural numbers, the same number of rational numbers as whole and even numbers, and infinitely more real numbers than whole, rational, or even numbers. Despite the initial rejection and criticism of his work, Cantor's discoveries fundamentally changed the understanding of infinity in mathematics. His work shows that there are an infinite number of sizes of infinity representing a range of infinite sets. Cantor's exploration of infinity and set theory took over twenty years, and his findings are now an integral part of our understanding of mathematics and its representation of reality.
  • Cantor's Diagonalization Argument: Cantor developed a famous argument known as the diagonalization argument. This involves comparing infinite sets by matching each element of one to another. If you can match up each element from one infinite set to another, then they have the same magnitude of infinity. If not, Cantor claimed that this proves there are different sizes of infinity.
  • Cantor's religious beliefs and their impact: As a devout Orthodox Christian, Cantor deeply believed that he was penetrating the mind of God through his study of infinities. He named the infinity of all infinities the 'Absolute Infinite' and equated this concept with God. This connected with his mathematical findings that an infinite subset could be equal to its whole set, a concept he saw as echoing the divine presence within all elements of the universe.
  • Rejection and mental health issues: Cantor's groundbreaking ideas were met with rejection from his peers in academia, leading to his ostracization. His religious contemporaries denounced him as a pantheist, causing deeply distressing personal conflict. Cantor's depression led to mental breakdowns and he spent his final years in an asylum.
  • Integrating Enlightenment and Mathematic Understanding: Cantor's struggles could stem from a possible failure to integrate his direct insights or glimpses of enlightenment with Orthodox beliefs. An alternative view suggests Cantor reached an advanced level of conceptualizing absolute infinity, without achieving a direct experience of it. This intense focus on conceptualizing could lead to mental breakdown if not balanced with experiential understanding.
  • Risk of Over Conceptualization: The dangers of over-conceptualizing absolute infinity without adequately balancing conceptual understanding with experiential understanding is demonstrated. This offers a valuable lesson to individuals pursuing deep understanding of aspects of reality through conceptual means to recognize the limits of this method. Failure to do so may result in mental instability.
  • Famous Mathematicians and Depression: The story of Cantor follows the trend in which famous mathematicians, physicists, and authors become severely depressive or succumb to mental instability. This is posited to be due to their overindulgence in deep conceptualization without gaining direct experience or knowledge of meditation and self-inquiry as an alternative approach.
  • Georg Cantor's understanding of Absolute Infinity: Georg Cantor, a mathematician, classified accessing infinity into three distinct categories. First, he talks about direct accessibility, which could be termed 'enlightenment'. Second, is that everything around us is infinite which reveals smaller infinities within the context of 'Absolute Infinity'. The third way to comprehend infinity is through abstract thoughts, as a form of mathematical formula or proof. Cantor didn't limit his understanding of infinity as a theoretical concept but portrayed that everything in the world can be divided into an infinite number of smaller infinities.
  • Georg Cantor's Block with mainstream acceptance: Despite having revolutionary ideas, Cantor faced severe backlash from mainstream science and mathematics bodies. While fighting for his beliefs, he had to suffer mental breakdowns and was not able to experience the full depth of enlightenment he aimed to achieve. His ideas gained mainstream acceptance several decades posthumously, highlighting the danger and barriers of a paradigm shift.
  • Giordano Bruno's Contribution to Infinity: Giordano Bruno (1550-1600 AD), a Dominican monk, mystic, and an avid believer in the works of Copernicus and Galileo, went beyond his contemporaries during the Scientific Revolution. He authored "The Infinite Universe and Worlds", where he proposed the existence of innumerable suns, with Earth-like planets orbiting them, teeming with life. He thoroughly believed in the concept of 'Absolute Infinity' pervading the universe.
  • Giordano Bruno's struggle with orthodoxy: Given the heretical nature of his teachings for his time, Bruno faced severe consequences. Professionally ostracized by the Orthodox Church, he was accused of heresy and pantheism. His insistence on promoting his radical ideas led to him being burned at the stake by the Roman Inquisition in 1600 AD. Bruno's fate is testament to the dangers of challenging established paradigms.
  • The Threat of Paradigm Locks: The fates of Georg Cantor and Giordano Bruno elucidate the challenges associated with defying established societal norms and paradigms.
