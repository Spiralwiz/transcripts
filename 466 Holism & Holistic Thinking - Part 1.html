Holism & Holistic Thinking - Part 1

  • Holistic thinking and holism: Everything in the universe is interconnected and cannot be understood by examining its parts in isolation. Key elements to this approach include considering relationships, interconnections, flow, merge, and how objects intertwine.
  • Dangers of reductionism and materialistic paradigm in science: Reductionism sees nature as a collection of discrete individual objects and is part of the materialistic paradigm prevalent in scientific fields. It assumes reality can be reduced to its most basic elements, such as atoms. This approach is unholistic, leading to misunderstandings and dysfunctions.
  • Implications of reductionist science: Reductionist attempts to understand complex realities, e.g., a frog, by studying it in isolation, disconnected from its environment, results in a limited understanding of the creature. The scientist's presence and perspective also influence the creature being studied, an entanglement problem underestimated by many scientists.
  • Quality of scientific research: The quality of science improves when conducted more holistically. However, high-quality holistic science is more expensive and time-consuming, making reductionist approaches more attractive for practical reasons. The use of convenient research subjects, like college students, embodies this issue.
  • Definition of holism: Dictionary definition of holism states that it is the theory stating that parts of a whole are so closely intertwined that they can't exist independently of the whole or be comprehended without reference to the whole.
  • Bias in Scientific Research: Universities often use undergraduate students as subjects for research and experiments. However, this represents a narrow, unrepresentative sample of the broader population. Majority of these students are western, affluent, white, non-religious, liberal, progressive, and within a specific age range, thus limiting the value of research findings as it can't be accurately extrapolated across the human race.
  • Misrepresentation in Meditation Studies: If scientists wish to study meditation, using undergraduate students would lead to skewed results since these students typically lack advanced meditation skills. To truly understand the effects of meditation, researchers would need to study advanced practitioners, such as yogis in India who have been meditating their entire lives, while also considering cultural and geographical diversity.
  • Reductionistic Analytical Thinking in Institutions: Most institutions, including academia, science, medicine, business, and government, have a bias towards analytical and reductionistic thinking. This approach focuses on dividing and categorizing reality rather than seeing the inherent interconnectedness of different elements. There is an overemphasis on narrow specializations, leading to a lack of broader understanding or wisdom.
  • Negative Consequences of Narrow Specializations: The focus on narrow specializations and technical expertise without the balance of holistic understanding leads to broader societal problems. Highly specialized professionals may seem authoritative, but their expertise is narrow and unholistic, leading to potential harm and unexpected consequences in the broader system they are a part of.
  • Problem in Medical and Pharmaceutical Industry: The reductionistic approach in the medical and pharmaceutical industries can be harmful. For instance, developing a drug to reduce heart disease without considering other potential side effects could lead to other severe health problems. Even if a drug doesn't harm the body, it could still affect the mind, potentially leading to depression and addiction. Therefore, it is crucial to have a holistic understanding when developing and administrating drugs.
  • Importance of Holism: Holism is essential in understanding the complexity and interconnectedness of reality. Without it, we are prone to making decisions based on a narrow and limited perspective, which can result in significant harm. An approach that elevates holistic understanding over narrow expertise is necessary to solve complex problems effectively.
  • Lack of Holistic Synthesizers in Society: Our society currently lacks holistic synthesizers - individuals capable of connecting different elements and identifying patterns. Current institutions inclined towards reductionistic thinking need to evolve towards a more holistic approach to generate creative solutions to complex societal issues. An understanding broader than one's field of specialty makes it easier to navigate unexpected changes that can drastically affect established theories and practices.
  • Reductionistic Approach in Healthcare: Leo Gura discusses how a reductionistic approach in healthcare, particularly in the pharmaceutical industry, has significant limitations and potential harms. For instance, a CEO might instruct scientists to develop a drug that reduces cholesterol, ignoring potential side effects or impacts on other parts of the body. This lack of holistic thinking can lead to unintended consequences, such as addiction and depression. Additionally, problems can arise from how the drug is marketed and priced, potentially causing harm to individuals and communities.
  • Perverse Incentives for Unholistic Thinking: Gura suggests that there is a perverse incentive for people, particularly those in leadership or business roles, to deliberately think in an unholistic manner. This approach often serves their own personal or financial interests at the cost of a wider societal impact. As one becomes more holistic, they have to take more responsibility for the consequences of their actions, making some decisions challenging.
  • Mismanagement and Lack of Holistic Vision: Gura cites the mishandling of the Star Wars trilogy by Disney as an example of the problems that arise due to a lack of a holistic vision. The original movies were created with an overarching, holistic vision by a single visionary. In contrast, Disney's approach lacked an overarching vision, resulting in a disjointed and unsatisfactory narrative.
  • The Importance of Holism in Chess: Gura uses the example of a chess game to illustrate the importance of holistic thinking. The most successful players are those who have a holistic understanding of the game and can plan several moves ahead, even making sacrifices for an ultimate win. Chess, in this sense, is a battle for holistic understanding.
  • The Consequences of Unholistic thinking: Gura suggests that a lack of holism can result in individuals painting themselves into corners, both in larger societal contexts and personal life. He describes this as essentially falling into potential problems or traps due to a limited perspective.
