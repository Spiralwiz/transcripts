How Openmindedness Works - Exercises To Open Your Mind

  • Understanding Open-mindedness: Open-mindedness serves as a crucial key in understanding reality within every aspect of life Â– self, emotions, humanity, society, reality, science, religion, spirituality, metaphysics, and philosophy. It is not a binary state but a spectrum of degrees, varying from person to person and even topic to topic for the same person. Open-mindedness and closed-mindedness both have distinct feelings associated with them, with each individual capable of experiencing both states.
  • Open vs Closed Minds: People can generally be categorized as having open or closed minds, with each classification demonstrating specific characteristics. Closed minds often manifest defensiveness, resistance, righteousness, and feeling threatened or offended, as well as justifying, rationalizing, and projection. These defenses mechanisms can result from attachment to an existing belief or ideology. On the other hand, open-mindedness signifies the willingness to consider alternative viewpoints or possibilities, even if they significantly diverge from societal norms and personal paradigms.
  • Experiential Learning: A practical exercise of imagining a scenario contrary to established beliefs, such as your mother hatching from an egg, can highlight the resistance, defensiveness, and refusal to let go of known 'facts' indicative of closed-mindedness. Exercising the mind in this way enables individuals to better recognize their reactions, patterns, and levels of open-mindedness versus closed-mindedness in various circumstances.
  • Radical Open-mindedness: Radical open-mindedness goes beyond general open-mindedness, requiring continuous self-evaluation and consideration of different viewpoints, no matter how extreme or contrasting they may be. Implementing open-mindedness on a micro level - examining and reconsidering every belief, idea, or position you hold - is emphasized over merely being open-minded on a macro level.
  • Engaging with Possibilities: The crucial aspect of open-mindedness is the ability and willingness to engage with new, even radical, possibilities or ideas without immediately dismissing them. Expectedly, the mind will resist and react defensively, manifesting as skepticism, ridicule, demands for proof, or counter-arguments, but it is critical to recognize and observe these reactions to understand one's mindset better.
  • Truth through Open-mindedness: Open-mindedness allows one to be continuously ready and welcoming of new perspectives and facts, enabling the exploration of reality in its full depth. It suggests a capacity for mental flexibility, independent thought, and the courage to let go of preconceived beliefs, eventually leading to enhanced understanding and interpretation of truth.
  • Understanding open-mindedness and closed-mindedness: Open-mindedness feels light and free, with a curiosity to explore new possibilities, while closed-mindedness feels resistant and uncomfortable. The ability to distinguish between these states in one's mind is a critical metaskill in navigating different domains and understanding the truth. This skill does not depend on the specific content of what is being considered.
  • Importance of open-mindedness before evaluating proof: Open-mindedness must come before accepting and evaluating proof and evidence. If someone is not open-minded in the first place, they won't fairly consider or give a chance to the evidence provided. This is a common mistake made by skeptical and rationally minded individuals who believe they only need to be open-minded when there is sufficient proof. Open-mindedness is about being willing to consider new possibilities without getting emotionally attached to them.
  • Examples from history: The progression of scientific understanding often requires an initial open-mindedness among scientists. For example, it took an open mind to question the widely accepted belief that the earth was stationary and to consider the possibility that it was moving. Even though the direct experience and perceived evidence of people at the time suggested that the earth was stationary, some philosophers and intellectuals had the open-mindedness to question this. It's essential to be open-minded first and then consider the proof and evidence for the most effective and truthful understanding of various domains.
  • Close-mindedness can be right, open-mindedness can be wrong: It's crucial to note that being close-minded doesn't necessarily mean being wrong. Similarly, being open-minded doesn't automatically translate to being accurate. For example, people can be close-minded about the concept of a flat earth, refusing to even consider it, and they might be right since the planet is round. The objective here isn't about being right or wrong but about having the ability to explore different perspectives with an open mind. Being open-minded is about exploring ideas, not sticking to a particular belief.
  • Ensuring understanding of the exercise: When doing exercises to enhance open-mindedness, some people may not get an experiential feel for the distinction between an open and closed mind. In such a case, it might be required to pause and find other examples that test intellectual positions one is unwilling to open their mind to. This will give a tangible sense of resistance and close-mindedness.
  • Feelings associated with close-mindedness and open-mindedness: Close-mindedness leaves one feeling uncomfortable, resistant, and unclean. On the other hand, open-mindedness is associated with feelings of lightness, calmness, freedom, curiosity, and an innate desire to explore new possibilities without an attachment to old positions. It's important not to get lost in the content of the examples and understand that open-mindedness is a metaskill about the structure of thinking, not the content of thought.
  • Imagining different perspectives: An open-minded individual would consider different perspectives without dismissing them outright based on their preconceived beliefs. For instance, the idea that one's mother hatched from an egg might seem ridiculous, but an open-minded person might consider it as an interesting proposition, prompting further curiosity.
  • Open-mindedness as a metaskill: Being open-minded or closed-minded is not dependent on specific facts or the truthfulness of a particular statement. It's a general skill one's mind can develop. Some minds are more open and can let go of old positions and explore new ones without emotional attachment. This skill, which is independent of the specifics being considered, allows for effective and truthful navigation across different domains like religion, science, politics, and much more.
  • The importance of open-mindedness over being factually correct: Leo Gura emphasizes that being open-minded is more crucial than being right as open-mindedness is an attitude that aids in various aspects of life. Individuals may be right in certain circumstances, but being open-minded allows people to overcome any mistakes or inaccuracies they may encounter. On the other hand, a closed-minded person sticks to their mistakes and inaccurate beliefs.
  • Consequences of closed-mindedness: Leo discusses how denial can be a powerful psychological force that can prevent one from accepting the truth. This is common among individuals, like scientists and skeptics, who believe that truth will prove itself to them, regardless of their skepticism. This attitude eliminates the crucial role that psychology plays in discovering truth. People can deny the reality and ignore the evidence contradictory to their beliefs due to their lack of open-mindedness.
  • Experimentation requires open-mindedness: One must be open-minded to engage in exploration and experimentation, both of which are important to gather evidence and prove a theory. Denial and closed-mindedness can prevent someone from accepting obvious facts about reality. Proof often requires effort, time, and resources, which many people are unwilling to invest in, especially when they assume the theory is impossible or highly unlikely.
  • Dogmatic belief and denial of alternative possibilities: Stubborn adherence to a belief can prevent one from exploring the truth further. This is exemplified by individuals who firmly believe in their religion or in the objectivity of reality. For them to progress further in understanding the truth, they must open themselves to alternatives like the possibility of God not being a myth or that reality could just be a product of one's imagination.
  • Examples of open-mindedness: Leo encourages the audience to consider outlandish examples like the possibility of Australia being a hoax or that Donald Trump could actually be a secret genius to test their openness to alternative ideas. The exercise aims to make viewers aware of their resistance and emotional reactions to these divergent ideas.
  • The importance of open-mindedness: Open-mindedness facilitates the exploration of alternative possibilities and realities, enabling a clearer understanding of the world. It involves considering various possibilities, however radical they might seem, and observing the emotions, resistance, or closures that they elicit. Moreover, truthful open-mindedness isn't tied to whether something is true, and phenomena don't have to be factual for one to open their mind to them.
  • The practice of open-mindedness and personal growth: Open-mindedness could lead to radical personal transformation, such as the possibility of becoming a millionaire or attracting a beautiful partner. It requires setting aside excuses and resistance and confronting shifting paradigms with courage.
  • Questioning religious beliefs and the existence of God: Open-mindedness entails entertaining the possibility that one's religious beliefs or the existence of God might be mistaken, regardless of societal or institutional taboos. This process can be emotionally daunting, especially if core established beliefs are challenged or undermined.
  • Examining societal establishments and norms: The practice of open-mindedness also involves considering that societal establishments like science, atheism, or a person's country could fundamentally be flawed or erroneous. This requires acknowledging the possibility that accepted facts or narratives could be mistaken and necessitates a radical reassessment of the worldview.
  • Experimenting with controversial ideas: Open-mindedness entails engaging with controversial notions such as reincarnation as a rapist, Hitler being a force for good in the world, or Buddha not reaching enlightenment. It involves considering these ideas objectively, without accepting them as truths.
  • Disassociating personal beliefs from the truth: The practice of open-mindedness involves understanding the difference between personal beliefs and actual reality. It shows that what people see as truth is often distorted by their biases, preconceived notions, and subjective needs. It requires acknowledging that the mind can skew perceptions to match the constructed reality.
  • The danger of loose-mindedness: The argument against open-mindedness due to the potential threat of turning into a religious fanatic, cult follower, or endorsing false claims such as flat-earth theory, is refuted. Such people are considered closed-minded, holding rigidly to their beliefs. Open-mindedness is about exploring and considering alternate views, not accepting one radical idea as the ultimate truth.
  • Understanding closed-mindedness: Ownership of closed-minded beliefs is frequently seen in individuals with rigid doctrines such as religious fundamentalists, nationalists, racists, cult leaders, and others who negate alternate perspectives. Recognizing these patterns is a critical first step in developing an open mindset.
  • Understanding Open-mindedness and Closed-mindedness: Open-mindedness supports intellectual curiosity, flexibility, and fearlessness. It is driven by an ability to detach and explore without prejudice or bias. Closed-mindedness, on the other hand, arises from fear, attachment, survival instincts, and ego. It represents a rigid mindset resistant to change. The willingness to explore new perspectives and consider different ways of understanding the world defines open-mindedness.
  • Implications of a Rigid Mind: The rigidity of the mind limits our ability to embrace new interpretations of reality, leading to a host of societal and personal problems in politics, religion, science, and business. Such rigidity can negatively impact relationships, emotions, finances, and careers.
  • Cultural Accumulation and Open-mindedness: The mind develops its core beliefs and assumptions based on the cultural environment it grows up in. This 'cultural accumulation' forms the bulk of our worldview, interactions, and decisions. The challenge lies in changing these core beliefs, which can greatly impact existing life situations and relationships.
  • Consequences of Changing Worldviews: Changing worldviews can have tangible and impactful changes in an individual's life, affecting relationships, living situations, and even career paths. Therefore, people are typically resistant to fundamentally shifting their perspectives due to fear of these consequences.
  • Radical Open-mindedness and Flexibility of Mind: Embracing radical open-mindedness and mental flexibility allows one to explore different perspectives without adhering rigidly to any specific one. This process, however, may take time and yield no immediate payoff, requiring a commitment to constant self-examination and exploration.
  • Perils of Blindly Following One Viewpoint: Unquestioning adherence to a single viewpoint can potentially lead to a biased and limited understanding of reality, even when following enlightened teachers. Remaining intellectually curious and open-minded to different possibilities mitigates this risk.
  • Cost of Open-minded Exploration: Exploring different perspectives involves time, energy, and confusion. This exploration doesn't offer immediate certainty or resolution. Despite these costs, maintaining an open-minded approach is crucial to gain a broader comprehension of reality.
  • Importance of question-driven exploration in open-mindedness: Leo Gura explains that simply adhering to predetermined beliefs or assumptions equates to a lack of mental exploration, discouraging open-mindedness and promoting closed-mindedness. This can lead to a distorted and untruthful view of reality.
  • Understanding the challenge in shifting perspectives: Gura discusses the challenges an individual may face when attempting to shift between different points of view, particularly noting the confusion, disorientation, and uncertainties that may arise. He emphasizes that this ability to switch perspectives rapidly is rare, and those lacking it are fundamentally closed-minded.
  • Recognizing closed-minded behavior: He stresses on the importance of self-awareness and reflection, urging viewers to recognize instances where their mind reacts defensively and closes off to alternative perspectives during ideological debates or arguments.
  • Attachment to beliefs and survival: Gura states that letting go of a belief or worldview is often perceived as a threat to an individual's survival, and this fear of loss can result in closed-mindedness. This is especially true when a belief becomes a part of the person's identity, leading to an unwillingness to question or abandon said belief.
  • Mind's role in constructing reality: He proposes that the mind is continuously involved in the construction of reality and its rigidity aids in the effective construction process. However, the mind denies its role in constructing reality to maintain a concrete sense of it. Acknowledging the role of one's mind in constructing reality could lead to realizing its illusory nature.
  • Adopting a new view of reality: Gura underscores the importance of recognizing the constructed nature of reality, postulating that being aware of the mind's role in this construction would lead to viewing reality as a creation, rather than an unchanging fact. This realization constitutes an integral part of being open-minded.
  • Accepting responsibility: Leo Gura discusses how by accepting that individuals are the creators of their own reality, they must also acknowledge they are responsible for their own suffering, negative emotions, and life situations. This can be a challenging notion to accept, leading to denial and a lack of awareness about this process.
  • Paradigm lock: Every worldview or belief system has a built-in mechanism that keeps people locked into that perspective. This usually involves instilling fear of the consequences of abandoning that belief. This characteristic is prevalent in cults, religions, science, logic, culture, capitalism, and even one's conception of sanity, with each paradigm using fear as an anchoring point.
  • Questioning paradigms: True open-mindedness requires the ability to recognize and overcome these locked paradigms by questioning and challenging them. This involves moving away from the fear-induced bounds and taking risks by exploring new and different perspectives and paradigms regardless of potential societal reactions or personal discomfort.
  • Self-deception and closed-mindedness: Many people mistakenly believe they are open-minded when in reality they are only open to information or ideas that reinforce their current beliefs. The mechanisms of self-deception play a key role in this process, as people unconsciously lean into their biases to protect themselves from the discomfort of challenging their belief systems. Real open-mindedness requires a willingness to question and potentially disregard existing beliefs.
  • Criteria for valid proof and evidence: Leo suggests that the criteria for what constitutes as valid proof and evidence are relative and subjective, with each individual's paradigm shaping what they consider as valid. True open-mindedness would require a willingness to consider proof and evidence outside of one's set criteria for validity.
  • Identity of open-mindedness: Leo warns against adopting the identity of being open-minded while not actually demonstrating open-minded behavior. The self-deception takes place when individuals believe they are open-minded but remain resistant to questioning and revising the core of their paradigms. He suggests such individuals are only open to surface-level changes and not to fundamental and structural changes.
  • Importance of tempered open-mindedness: It's critical to temper open-mindedness with grounding in direct experience and honesty. Open-mindedness isn't about blindly believing every suggestion out there, but about being willing to explore different perspectives without losing touch with actual experiences.
  • Vulnerabilities of extreme open-mindedness: If not tempered with honesty and grounded experiences, open-mindedness can lead to belief in far-fetched theories. People might start believing in improbable concepts absent from their direct experiences, leading to distractions or disillusionments.
  • The deception of defensive arguments: If a person is truly aware of the truth, there would be no need for defensiveness or striving to prove others wrong. The truth requires no defending, and any markers of defensiveness, arrogance, and over-confidence indicate a lack of true knowledge.
  • Open-mindedness and honesty: It's crucial to be honest about what exists in direct conscious experience. If claimed knowledge or beliefs aren't part of one's immediate awareness or experience, one must admit ignorance rather than blindly accepting them.
  • Influence of self-deception: Understanding one's self-deception is crucial to unravelling the nature of life and reality. Self biases and survival agendas can lead to self-deception, where the mind shuts down to protect these biases from truth. This self-deception often manifests as closed-mindedness.
  • Implications of closed-mindedness: A closed mind acts like a barrier, preventing individuals from exploring and understanding the vast realms of knowledge beyond their limited worldviews. Despite the initial discomfort, dismantling these barriers and challenging self-biased beliefs can lead to profound growth and understanding.
  • Destabilising implications of open-mindedness: Open-mindedness can significantly shake the foundations of one's constructed reality. Questioning deep-seated beliefs and worldviews can lead to their dismantlement, inducing big changes in the perception of reality.
  • Effects of intellectual attachment: People often have strong attachments to their ideas, beliefs, and worldviews, which drastically influence their perception of reality. Letting go of these intellectual attachments can have a profound impact on understanding and interpreting reality.
  • The balance between open-mindedness and grounded reality: While open-mindedness plays a crucial role in intellectual exploration, it is necessary to balance it with one's grounded reality to avoid delving into improbable theories or fantasies. It's essential to stay honest about what lies within one's direct experiences.
  • On use of open-mindedness in spreading ideologies and conspiracy theories: Open-mindedness should not be used as a vehicle for wild speculation, guesswork, or us-versus-them fear-based conspiracy theories. Having different perspectives does not mean subscribing to fear-based concepts. Egocentricity tends to co-opt open-mindedness to spread its own ideologies and to fear monger.
  • On maintaining grounded open-mindedness: Open-mindedness entails consistency in evaluating and weighing different perspectives and theories. While it can be emotionally challenging due to the accompanying uncertainties, it's vital to stay grounded in direct and true experiences, denying space to wild speculations and projections.
  • On changing perspectives originating from self-deception: Understanding open-mindedness as a specter rather than a binary distinction aids in realization of personal biases. Regular self-evaluation and observation of personal close-mindedness can offer valuable lessons about the nature and falls of egocentric biases.
  • On challenging rigid beliefs and cultivating open-mindedness: Open-mindedness demands continually assessing oneself and accepting the alternative viewpoints that it unravels. To foster this ability in others, one useful strategy is to stimulate their imagination to consider perspectives subtly beyond their current paradigm.
  • On the power of imagination in opening minds: Instead of confronting somebody with a different viewpoint directly, using subtle exercises of imagination can expand perspectives and open minds more effectively. For instance, asking a person to consider how their believes may have been shaped had they been born somewhere else, or to envision an alternative outcome based on a new possibility, can challenge and shift their current paradigm.
  • Understanding Closed-Mindedness and Leveraging Imagination: Closed-mindedness functions to prevent imagination, limiting the ability to perceive alternatives outside one's established beliefs or paradigm. However, imagination is a powerful tool, capable of expanding these boundaries. Guiding individuals through detailed, emotionally compelling, imaginative scenarios can open their minds to new perspectives.
  • Influence and Communication Through Imagination: The vivid and compelling use of imagination can persuade, motivate, and lead people. Using this tool to guide people through the process of imagining alternative beliefs can open them up and break down ideological barriers. Public speakers, leaders, conversationalists, or anyone aiming to convince others can successfully employ imagination to create a strong, positive vision of the future.
  • Imagination as a Tool for Growth: The ability to vividly imagine new possibilities can inspire action and drive personal growth. For example, having a clear, compelling vision of taking a vacation in Australia makes it more likely that one would actually purchase a ticket and go. Therefore, imagination can challenge closed-mindedness and introduce new ideas and understanding.
  • Open-mindedness and the Concept of Infinity: Open-mindedness can lead to realizing the concept of infinity, and the possibility of an unlimited, completely self-aware consciousness. By stepping beyond the confines of finite, limited understanding, one can open up to the possibility of a completely omniscient, all-powerful, completely beautiful, and perfect reality.
  • Open-mindedness as a Daily Practice: Open-mindedness is a skill that can be practiced and developed. Noticing when the mind is closing down during conversations, while watching the news or in the middle of disagreements, and consciously practice opening it up, is a valuable exercise.
  • Significance of Open-mindedness and Nurturing it: Open-mindedness has the potential to change life trajectories and lead to profound personal growth, akin to planting a seed that grows into a fruitful tree over time. Honing and nurturing this open-mindedness can yield rich and rewarding fruits that can change the life of an individual. The potential transformation resulting from open-mindedness far exceeds any boundaries set by science, Western philosophy, or personal presumptions. It requires ongoing watering and nurturing but can result in limitless opportunities for growth and understanding.
