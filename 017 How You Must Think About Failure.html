How You Must Think About Failure

  • Reframing the Perception of Failure: Leo Gura proposes a shift in the popular mindset from "failure equals bad" to "failure equals good." He argues that failure is part of a larger learning process and crucial for achieving progress and a deeper understanding of various domains of life.
  • The Role of Social Conditioning: The negative view on failure is largely due to social conditioning where mistakes are punished, thus conditioning fear of failure from a young age. This mindset hampers individual progress, innovative thinking, and the natural learning process that includes making mistakes.
  • Necessity of Both Success and Failure: Leo insists that desirable learning and progression beyond initial limitations require a blend of both successes and failures. By rejecting failure and only pursuing success, individuals limit their growth potential.
  • The Concept of Reality as a "Possibility Space": Gura describes reality as a broad scope of possibilities, the results of which depend entirely on the actions taken. This view supports the idea of adopting an explorer's mindset, navigating through this "possibility space" to discover what works and what doesn't.
  • The Explorer's Mindset: Leo proposes adopting the mindset of an explorer as opposed to a fixed-path follower. This alternative mindset empowers individuals to test diverse routes and seize potential opportunities that would otherwise be overlooked by traditional success-driven individuals. Learning to manage shifts and trials along the journey is crucial to ultimate success.
  • Mastering Different Domains: The process of exploring and experimenting without being attached to a specific outcome is a way of gaining mastery in different fields. By focusing on the journey and not just the end goal, there is more potential for improvement, adaptability, and overall success.
  • The Fear of Making Mistakes: Fear arises from the desire to be efficient and effective, which can cause doubts and hesitations when faced with new challenges. The natural urge to reach the intended goal straightaway leads to a belief that any deviation or mistake is a failure. This mindset, termed as the 'failure equals bad' mentality, inhibits personal growth and potential to learn from mistakes.
  • The Mechanism of Trial and Error: Success generally involves a process of trial and error. Emphasis is laid on the importance of having a broad understanding of different industries and approaches for entrepreneurial success, and of viewing failures as part of the larger context rather than as overwhelming setbacks. An experimentation approach is encouraged even if it might lead to perceived failure, as it contributes to learning and deep skill development.
  • Navigating Low and High-Cost Failures: Leo differentiates between low-cost, high-return failures and high-cost, low-return failures. For instance, while learning a new skill such as Photoshop or cooking, the possible mistakes are seen as low-cost failures, but the return in terms of learning is high. High-cost failures with little return could be risking life savings in a casino. Emphasis is put on seeking strategic failures that provide valuable learning experiences without significant costs.
  • Evolving through Reference Experiences: The transcript highlights how 'reference experiences' or impactful events can shape one's understanding and behavior over time. Gura advises actively seeking reference experiences specific to one's goals, even if they involve failure, as long as the cost is minimal.
  • Coaching and the Organic Process: As a coach himself, Leo Gura advises coaches to remain open to guiding their clients organically, trying different approaches, being responsive to feedback, and not fearing mistakes. He talks about his own learning experience through trial and error, advocating for long-term growth and learning over immediate results. The approach of seeing failures as learning opportunities is stressed for all, not just coaches.
  • Change in mindset towards failure: Instead of viewing failure as a deterrent, one should adopt a mindset of exploration. This allows individuals to try various approaches, even if they have a high probability of failure, as they can offer valuable experiences and insights. Success is not the ultimate goal here; rather, it's about understanding and mastering the various aspects of life.
  • Concept of breadth of experiences: By exploring various paths instead of focusing solely on one's goal, you gain a breadth of experiences which contribute to flexibility and ultimately lead to success. This mitigates the disappointment felt when a focused approach leads to a dead end.
  • Repeated failure leading to mastery: Many successful people attribute their achievements to their numerous failures. The process of trying, failing, and then trying again can lead to the discovery of previously unconsidered solutions or ideas. This is evident in Thomas Edison's countless trials while inventing the light bulb.
  • Robust understanding of a domain: Viewing failure as part of the exploration process can contribute to a deeper understanding of a domain. This robust understanding can be applied in different areas and lead to more versatile problem-solving abilities.
  • Failure builds emotional leverage: Failure can induce a level of frustration that motivates individuals to take real action and bring about significant change in their lives. This is especially relevant in scenarios where repeated failures spur individuals to identify and address underlying issues that prevent them from achieving their goals.
  • Shifting from a gold miner to an explorer mindset: The video encourages viewers to adopt an exploratory approach, akin to an explorer, as opposed to a "gold miner" who is desperately outcome-focused. This mindset acknowledges failure as part of the learning process and alleviates the desperation associated with achieving immediate success.
  • Implementation of the mindset: Internalizing and operationalizing the mindset shift towards seeing failure as a learning opportunity is crucial. This process starts with awareness and recognizing one's existing approach towards dealing with failure.
  • Explorer vs Gold miner mindset: Leo Gura discusses the difference between an 'explorer' versus a 'gold miner' mindset when pursuing goals. The explorer is driven by curiosity and has a desire to explore without a rigid outcome dependency, whereas the gold miner is overly focused, often desperately, on a specific outcome.
  • Christopher Columbus example: Using the example of Christopher Columbus, Gura demonstrates how Columbus' obsession with finding a shortcut to the Spice Islands hindered his ability to appreciate the significant value of the new continent he discovered. Columbus was so fixated on his intended objective that he overlooked unexpected, valuable discoveries.
  • Adoption of Explorer mindset: Gura suggests that adopting an explorer mindset is more fruitful for long-term success. This mindset brings benefits such as deep understanding, joy of exploration, and natural curiosity while relieving the pressure to be perfect. It encourages perseverance, courage, and confidence in eventual success, no matter the circumstances.
  • Business start-up analogy: Gura uses starting a business as an analogy to highlight the benefits of adopting an explorer mindset. He emphasizes the importance of being open to exploration, conducting tests and experiments, and adapting strategies based on market response. Gura suggests that it's essential to cater to the market's needs, even if it means changing one's original business plan.
  • Mindset's impact on the learning process: Gura highlights that adopting the explorer mindset allows for more organic learning process, where mistakes are seen as learning opportunities rather than setbacks. This mindset cultivates agility, encourages adaptability, and leads to the discovery of unexpected opportunities.
  • Long-term perspective: Gura emphasizes the need to have a long-term perspective when pursuing goals, such as becoming a better entrepreneur, rather than just focusing on short-term wins like starting a successful business. He highlights the idea of robust success, which comes from starting multiple businesses, gaining varied experiences, and developing a deep understanding of different areas, rather than stumbling upon success in one domain.
  • Conclusion: Gura concludes by encouraging viewers to adopt an explorer mindset and to prioritize gaining a deep understanding of their domain over immediate results. He emphasizes the benefits of this approach, particularly in new areas of business, and argues that mistakes, setbacks, and even outright failures are all part of the learning process and should be embraced rather than feared.
  • Fear of failure creates fragile success: Success built upon fear of failure rather than understanding the entire process is fragile. You may for example build a prosperous business but deep down realize that luck played a significant role in your success. As such, you'll live in constant fear and insecurity of losing it all, which could lead to irrational, paranoid decisions that may destroy your business. This fragility arises because you have only explored one path to success and thus limit your understanding of the vast market domain and possibilities to rebuild if your business fails.
  • Building a robust success: Building robust success involves tolerance of failure and a deep understanding of what you're mastering. This makes you confident and grounded, knowing you can rebuild from failure, unlike when success is based solely on dodging failure. Taking an experimental approach is better as it triggers learning which builds mental models for handling different scenarios successfully.
  • Differentiating types of failure: It's important to differentiate between low-cost, high-return failures and high-cost, low-return failures. Low-cost failures like messing up a meal or a canvas in photoshop provide important learning experiences at minimal cost. However, high-cost failures like betting all your life savings in a game offer little but heartache and regret.
  • Strategic failing gets you valuable reference experiences: Proactively looking for failure is the ultimate goal. The catch is to look for strategic failures, failuring in a way that it gives you plenty of valuable reference experiences without hurting too much in terms of money, energy, or time. With numerous reference experiences under your sleeve, you gain fresh perspectives about the world and understand how things work, which guides your future decision making.
  • Importance of reference experiences: The presenter discusses the impact of reference experiences in life, which come from powerful events that shape our understanding and behavior. They are created from critical moments such as near-death experiences, severe illnesses, and deep regrets which prompt us to rethink our lives, building emotional leverage and a deeper understanding. These experiences can sometimes cause drastic changes in behavior, as one might swing wildly from one extreme to another to avoid repeating a negative experience.
  • Proactively seeking reference experiences: Leo recommends actively seeking reference experiences related to specific skills or general life knowledge. He suggests purposely taking risks and tolerating failure if it leads to increased understanding and minimal costs. For example, while trying to improve at poker, one could invest a specific amount to participate in tournaments, learn valuable knowledge from losses, and potentially benefit in the future - a strategy that also applies to business.
  • Building an explorer mindset: The presenter discusses the need to nurture an 'explorer mindset.' He encourages viewers to recognize fear or hesitation in decision-making, evaluate the cost of potential mistakes, and slowly condition oneself to embrace exploration in situations where the cost of failure is low. This gradual process allows the development of an explorers attitude, reducing fear and promoting understanding.
  • Applying the explorer mindset in life coaching: Leo shares an example from his experience in a life coaching program, where he moved away from the results-oriented mindset of solving a client's problem and began to experiment with different techniques and approaches. This approach enhanced his coaching skills, improved his effectiveness in assisting clients, and fostered his confidence in coaching.
  • Benefits of the explorer mindset: The presenter emphasizes the benefits of adopting an explorer mindset in various areas of life, advocating for the change from viewing failure as negative to accepting it as a part of the learning process. He concludes by reminding viewers of the fun in discovering new things and the enhanced understanding that comes with this mentality, thereby making it an optimal tool for personal development and mastery.
